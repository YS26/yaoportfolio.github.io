LED Thermal Management Project

This study aims to design a fin-heat sink for a 20W Light Emitting Diode (LED) chip assembly. A fin-heat is a passive heat exchanger that transfers the heat from the LED. The assembly includes the LED, soldered to a Metal Core-Printed Circuit Board (MC-PCB), and adhered to the LED using a Thermal Interface Material (TIM). Assuming the chip should not exceed 95°C, 60% of the input power of the LED is converted to waste and indoor air of 25°C. In order to carry out this study a single pin fin was analyzed using a finite difference model and scaling that to a full pin array. Which served to determine the total effectiveness of the design, volume of the assemble, and the cost for materials and manufacturing.

Analysis of Vapor-Compression Refrigeration Systems

The purpose of this project is to design an optimum refrigeration system for a warehouse with both freezing and refrigerated spaces. To do this, three different refrigeration cycles were designed and analyzed. Each cycle followed the given guidelines. The input power required by the compressors and the coefficient of performance of the systems were compared to find the most efficient system. Parametric studies were performed to study the effects of the freezer temperature, the refrigerator temperature, and the ambient temperature on the coefficient of performance for each of the designed cycles.

Finite Element Analysis of forces acting on C-Clamp using SolidWorks

The goal of this project is to model and utilize static studies in determining the forces, stresses, axial compressions, shear stresses, torsions as well as hand calculations for the C-clamp parts. We performed these finite element analysis calculations in three parts: the body, screw and handle of the C-clamp. With our performed finite element analysis and calculations, we are then able to determine the maximum clamping force that the C-clamp can accommodate and determine if the stress in the clamp exceeds yield strength for the rated clamping force.

Dam Power Generation in Respect to Reservoir Height and Penstock Dimensions

Hydroelectric power is one of the most familiar and widespread substitutes for fossil fuels. Hydroelectric dams are scattered throughout the nation and generate about ten percent of Americas’ energy needs. Dams are designed and constructed under the supervision of engineers who seek to maximize hydroelectric energy production while also ensuring that the dam is structurally sound. This study seeks to determine how the height and area of a dam reservoir, and the properties of a penstock, affects the mechanical forces applied to a hydroelectric turbine, and how this mechanical force is converted into usable electric energy.
